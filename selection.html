Background on DevConf 2022 Review Process

This page is a retrospective guide on how the talks were selected for the upcoming DevConf conference. We hope it helps create community transparency of the process, and as a secondary goal, that it helps others prepare for submissions for other events including DevConf in the future. This is the community's event and as the organisers, we welcome all feedback and suggested improvements for how we can be better stewards of the community's event. Please feel free to email us at info@devconf.co.za about this or anything else about your event.

We made use of the sessionize platform to collect, review and respond to talk submissions. The call for speakers page is still available with all the information on it about how the process would be run. Sessionize has a free simulation which you can log into, with 10 fake submissions for you to review and rank if you would like to better understand the process that we followed.

It is important to note that DevConf uses an anonymous review process. All any of the reviewers see is the title, notes, description and track. Any other information supplied in fields other than those listed is not made visible to a reviewer. Even as organisers, we aren't able to see any additional information or personally identifiable information until after the review process is concluded.

We are using the team rank explicitly here as the talks were not scored or assigned a rating. They were ranked, i.e. first, second, third etc....

The ranking is based on the Elo rating system, a chess-rating system also used for multiplayer competitions in a number of video games, sports, board games, etc. You can read more about it on Wikipedia.
When the reviewer begins, each session has a 1500 rating.
A comparison starts with choosing a triple that will be compared. The first batch of triplets is chosen randomly until all sessions are compared at least once. Then things get smarter! Sessionize looks at comparison count, win count and rating difference to pick the next triplet while making sure that the reviewers never compare the same sessions twice.
Each comparison results in three 'games': A vs B, B vs C and C vs A. The result of each game can be a win, lose or draw. Each result changes the session's rating. The exact amount depends on the other session's current rating (as defined by Elo ranking system). Our K-factor ranges from 40 at the start to 10 towards the end of the evaluation.

For DevConf 2022, talks were anonymously ranked in Sessionize by a panel of 9 reviewers from diverse backgrounds of race, gender, age, experience, technology and disabilities. Each reviewer was either a previous speaker or reviewer of DevConf and thus has had a deep insight into what the conference's goals are and the type of event it should be. We did not provide any official guidance or offer reviewers a "marking guide" - rather we trusted them to bring their experience and discretion to the selection.
Reviewers could provide written feedback about a submission, but few did and even those who did provide feedback, did so sparingly. All the reviewers did this in their free time without any promise of compensation and spent tens of hours working on it so we did not require nor expect them to include comments with their reviews.
The average of the rankings assigned by the Elo score is then used to determine the final ranking.

Once that that was done, Candice and Robert built the agenda mostly from the ordered list (i.e. talks ranked 1 through 40 go in), with a few exceptions:

  • International speakers - Due to the risks for logistics and travel, we did not accept any international speakers. We hope to bring international speakers back in 2023. This was decided before the call for speakers was opened, and it was communicated on the call for speakers website.
  • A speaker with a selected talk would have all their other submissions dropped. This is to allow many diverse views and opportunities (one speaker sharing twice in a day vs. 2 speakers sharing once in a day) and allow speakers to spend time on the day with the audience and enjoy the event as well as to give them time to focus.
  • Duplication - If we already have a talk that covers the same ground, we won't select a second one. We could have talks on the same topic if it's clear that there is distinction and value for the audience.
  • Content that doesn't fit - We published a list of what is and isn't suitable content as part of the submission request and thankfully the ranking pushes these down so we have not had to drop any highly ranked inappropriate talks but that is a possibility.
  • Discretionary - As organisers, we need to ensure a balanced and relevant agenda, based entirely on community feedback provided over the years so that the conference continues to be a high-quality event. We apply discretion only to ensure our agenda suits our audience's varying interests. We are guided by reviewers' feedback, community feedback on social media and at meetups and other conferences and this discretion is applied exceptionally sparingly.
    A good example from this year is we had 2 similar talks in our top 20 in the 2021 reviews; the first talk was, in fact, the #1 ranked talk and was from an international speaker, the other was from a local speaker. We opted for the lower-ranked one as the local speaker had the advantage of relevance in our market (which was pertinant to the talk) and we were confident they would present and capture their audience just as well as the international speaker.
  • Where there are similar talks that rank similarly, we would allow the opportunity to underrepresented groups.
  • We also try to limit too much content in a particular theme - considering most of our submissions are about languages and tools, and many ranked in the top 12 - and this is to make room for talks in categories such as mental health, DevOps and Security, for example.

FAQ

How many talks are there?

We selected 29 talks from 217 submissions, meaning that only 13% of talks are selected.

What can I do to increase my chances for selection in future?

Write a great description. Your description needs to do a lot of work for you:
  • It needs to provide the detail of the content you are going to present potentially to a reviewer who isn't specifically an expert in it.
  • It needs to show you know what you are talking about.
  • It needs to have something to help it stand out.
This is difficult to do and talks a lot of writing and polishing to get it perfect. Attending the CFP workshops during the year that is run by the community is a very useful way to skill up here and this is why we partnered with other communities to run these for DevConf especially. A second tip is to engage in the community throughout the year - if you have an important topic, helping reviewers to know it is valuable ahead of conference time is invaluable.

Does being a speaker from a current or previous sponsor help?

Not at all. The process is anonymous so there is no way it can impact it. In addition, your event is run totally cost neutral with no profit being taken by the organisers so monatry influence is not possible.

Why did the rejection emails not contain feedback?

There are three factors that lead to a basic "sorry, you did not get selected email".
  • Sessionize has no way to automate giving even basic information (like rank for example) let alone feedback.
  • As stated reviewers did not leave feedback for the majority of talks, so even if there was a solution there would be no content.
  • It is not possible for Candice and Robert to write personal rejections in a timeframe that is respectful of speakers who would wait months for feedback this way.

Some people have received e-mails notifying them of acceptance, while others have not - is there an issue?

No. The process, as documented on the call for speakers, is that once we select the speakers they are given an opportunity to confirm. We have had a speaker or two not accept and in that case, we then go back to the submissions to identify the person next in line by ranking. Once we have all the 29 slots filled, we send out the declination emails.

There was not enough space in the description for me to get my talk across properly.

We are aware of how limited the space in the description and will be increasing it for future events.
